This document defines the core writing style and principles for all [COMPANY NAME] [PRODUCT CATEGORY] marketing content. All content generators should reference these guidelines to ensure consistency.

## Tone

**Direct** — Be short, punchy, action-first.

- ❌ "Our solution includes automation features designed to reduce time spent on administrative processes."
- ✅ "Automate the busywork. Focus on growth."

**Outcome-obsessed** — Tie everything to measurable results.

- ❌ "Powerful [product category] that drives business results."
- ✅ "Help your [audience] close more deals." / "[Specific outcome]."

**Opinionated** — Challenge the status quo when it’s true to the brand.

- ❌ "[Topic] is one way to measure effectiveness."
- ✅ "If you’re only measuring [old way], you’re already behind."

**Visionary** — Paint the big picture when it fits. Avoid bland, generic “opportunity” language.

- ❌ "The modern [category] landscape offers many opportunities for innovation."
- ✅ "This isn’t just a new way to [do X]. It’s a new way to compete."

## "AI speak" — Patterns to Catch and Remove

AI is part of the workflow. That doesn’t change the bar for what we publish. These guidelines keep our writing clear and human. They will evolve; the responsibility stays the same. **Catch weak patterns and remove them.** Common ones:

**❌ Giveaway words** — Overused, nonhuman-sounding. Cut or replace.

- *Examples to avoid:* delve, realm, ever-evolving, navigate, embark, leverage, synergy, robust, seamless, cutting-edge, world-class, best-in-class, holistic, ecosystem (when vague), empower (unless intentional), utilize, facilitate, streamline (unless specific)

**❌ Simple contrasts, often alliterative** — "It’s not X, it’s Y" can sound like a template.

- *Examples to avoid:* "It’s about data, not drama." / "It’s not learning, it’s transformation." Use this structure sparingly; when you do, make it specific and true to the brand.

**❌ Em dashes** — Use sparingly. Prefer a full stop, comma, or rephrasing. Overuse makes copy feel breathless and samey.

**❌ Overuse of transitions** — Sounds like an essay, not a human.

- *Examples to avoid:* In conclusion, To summarize, The truth is, At the end of the day, It’s important to note, Furthermore, Additionally (unless it genuinely helps)

**Rule:** If a phrase could appear in any competitor’s content, question it. Replace with something only [COMPANY NAME] would say.

## Core Writing Philosophy

**Write in a natural, human voice:** Your content should sound like a thoughtful colleague explaining something helpful, not a corporate press release or technical manual.

**Use everyday language:** Write like you're talking to a colleague. Use "use" instead of "utilize." Use "help" instead of "facilitate." Use words that feel natural, not forced.

**Be direct and clear:** Get to the point quickly. Every sentence should add value. Cut unnecessary words. If you can say it in five words instead of ten, do it.

**Vary your sentence structure:** Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones. This creates natural flow and prevents monotony.

**Use proper punctuation:** Curly quotes ("") and commas for natural pauses. Let punctuation guide the reader's breathing.

**Connect ideas smoothly:** Let one thought flow naturally into the next. Avoid forced transitions like "Furthermore" or "Additionally" unless they genuinely help.

## Key Principles

### 1. Human and Approachable

- Write like talking to a colleague
- Benefit-focused - emphasize what the product helps people accomplish
- Accessible language - write for [TARGET AUDIENCE], not technical experts
- Action-oriented - use natural, conversational language

**Use:**
- "helps you"
- "enables"
- "makes it easier"
- "reduces"
- "improves"

**Avoid:**
- "enabling"
- "empowering"
- "leveraging"
- "utilizing"
- "optimizing"

### 2. [CUSTOMER/CLIENT] as the Hero

Position the [CUSTOMER/CLIENT] as the hero. [PRODUCT NAME] is the tool enhancing their capabilities, not the star of the show.

**Examples:**
- ✅ "[Customer] increased revenue by 40% using [Product]"
- ❌ "[Product] helped [Customer] increase revenue by 40%"

### 3. Clear AND Clever (The Golden Ratio)

**Clarity is non-negotiable.** Never sacrifice understanding for creativity.

**But clever enhances clear.** When you can be both clear AND clever, that's where great content lives.

**Good clever:**
- Wordplay that lands with your audience
- Puns that illuminate the point
- Analogies that make complex ideas accessible
- Turns of phrase that resonate with the segment or persona

**Bad clever:**
- Obscure references that confuse
- Wordplay that distracts from the message
- Puns that require explanation
- Jokes that only you find funny

**The test:** If someone doesn't get it immediately, it's too clever. If it makes them smile while understanding faster, it's perfect.

### 4. Action-Oriented

Use natural, conversational language that drives action without being pushy or salesy.

**Examples:**
- ✅ "Get started in minutes"
- ✅ "See how it works"
- ❌ "Experience the power of our revolutionary solution"
- ❌ "Discover the transformative capabilities"

## [COMPANY NAME] Style Compliance

### Language & Grammar

- **American English spelling** and maintain consistency (e.g., "[example word]," not "[alternative spelling]")
- **Oxford comma** in all lists
- **Sentence case for titles** (not title case)
- **Active voice** preferred over passive voice
- **Contractions are OK** when they sound natural ("don't" instead of "do not")

### What to Avoid (Or Use Carefully)

**Idioms and turns of phrase:**
- Use if they resonate with your audience (e.g., "[relevant idiom]" for [specific segment])
- Skip if obscure or don't translate well (e.g., "[overused phrase]" is corporate speak)

**Acronyms and lingo:**
- Define on first use or avoid entirely
- Don't assume your audience knows industry-specific terms

**Generic buzzwords and "AI speak":**
- Don't use "[buzzword 1]," "[buzzword 2]," "[buzzword 3]," "[buzzword 4]" without explanation
- If you must use them, explain what they mean in context
- See the **"AI speak"** section above for giveaway words and patterns to catch (delve, realm, overused transitions, etc.)

**Corporate jargon:**
- Avoid "[jargon 1]," "[jargon 2]," "[jargon 3]," "[jargon 4]," "[jargon 5]"
- Unless genuinely needed, choose simpler alternatives

**Superlatives without proof:**
- "best," "fastest," "industry-leading" require substantiation
- Always back up strong claims with data or proof points

**Remember:** Clever language that enhances clarity = ✓. Clever language that requires explanation = X.

## Claim Substantiation

**CRITICAL:** All claims must be defensible and substantiated. This is a legal and brand protection requirement.

### Defensible Claims (Use These)

- **Operational Benefits:** "[Example 1]," "[Example 2]," "[Example 3]"
- **Customer Experience:** "[Example 1]," "[Example 2]," "[Example 3]"
- **General Comparisons:** "[Example 1]," "[Example 2]"
- **Verified Data:** Only use data from campaign briefs, case studies, or official sources

### X Avoid These Claims (Unless Verified)

- **Specific Rate Comparisons:** "[Example 1]," "[Example 2]," "[Example 3]"
- **Competitive Claims:** "[Example 1]," "[Example 2]"
- **Unverified Metrics:** Specific percentages, dollar amounts, or time savings without source data
- **Market Claims:** "[Example 1]," "[Example 2]," "[Example 3]" without substantiation
- **Invented Numbers:** No "[Example 1]," "[Example 2]," "[Example 3]" without proof

### Guidelines

- **When in doubt, be conservative** - It's better to understate than hyperbolic overstatements
- **Focus on operational benefits** rather than competitive comparisons
- **Use project/campaign data only** - Don't extrapolate or make assumptions
- **Question every quantitative claim** - Ask "Where did this number come from?"
- **Use general language** - "[General statement]" vs "[Specific claim]" (unless verified)
- **NEVER invent specific numbers** - No "[Example 1]," "[Example 2]," "[Example 3]" without explicit substantiation
- **Only use data explicitly provided** in source materials (campaign briefs, case studies, customer interviews)
